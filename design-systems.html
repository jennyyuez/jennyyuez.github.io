Case study — Design systems

Design systems at scale

When I joined Sitemate I was the sole product designer — and the design in Figma had drifted a long way from what was live in production. This is how I built the design system from the ground up, and then drove it into the codebase through Storybook.

Role
Product Designer · sole designer
Scope
Foundation build · then ongoing
Team
Design Systems · Engineering
Tools
Figma · Storybook · Chromatic
Disciplines
Design Systems · Documentation · Accessibility

The challenge

Figma said one thing.
Production said another.

One of the first things I noticed when I joined was the inconsistency between my designs and what was live in production. After discussions with the engineering team, it became clear the Figma design system and the component library were out of sync — many assets were outdated, and significant engineering effort would be needed to update the Bootstrap components in the codebase to match the design system.

I audited the product and the library, and the drift fell into four buckets:

Unregulated components
Components without a source of truth

Bootstrap components shipped with incorrect or improvised styling applied — each one a one-off decision nobody could trace back to a rule.

Inconsistent design
Same patterns, different pixels

Incorrect form sizing, inconsistent spacing, and button styles that varied screen to screen — eroding trust in the interface one detail at a time.

Inconsistent copy
Words that didn't agree with each other

Incorrect casing and inconsistent terminology across the product — small things that genuinely confuse users in a compliance tool.

Scarce guidelines
A library without a manual

Barely any documentation on design patterns within the library — so every new screen meant re-deriving decisions that should have been settled.

The approach

Right-sized, not over-engineered

Organising components at this scale was something I hadn't done before — so I studied how Atlassian, Google, and Apple structure theirs. The honest conclusion: their systems were far too complex for the stage our company was at.

What made the difference was keeping things simple and flexible by following atomic design principles. That gave me a system that was easy to use, could grow with the team, and supported early-stage product development without slowing releases.

How can I make a design system that's clear, easy to use, and scalable — for designers and developers?

— The question behind every decision

Scaling into code

From Figma library to Storybook

A design system only counts if engineering actually builds with it. As the Figma library matured, Storybook (with Chromatic) was introduced so engineers had a matching library of coded components — but over time the two sources began to deviate, and that friction was quietly affecting consistency and implementation.

I started with a component audit — the Badge component made the problem obvious. It had accumulated unnecessary variants that required double-handling to maintain, and engineering had structured it differently from the Figma library. Working with a senior engineer, we consolidated the variants, agreed a shared structure across both libraries, and introduced Dev Docs inside Storybook — implementation notes and considerations engineers document per component.

The other friction point was process: engineers who hadn't worked with Storybook before had no clear guidance, and product team members didn't know how to hand a Storybook ticket over. So I documented the whole pipeline and set clear expectations between Product and Engineering:

1
Charge the ticket properly

Every component ships with acceptance criteria — name, variants, and input values, plus behaviour states: default, active, disabled, error, loading.

2
Hand off to engineering

A recorded walkthrough of the design and acceptance criteria, so the engineer starts with context instead of a cold ticket — and knows exactly who to ask.

3
Review the built component

A demo review to test padding, structure, and behaviour against the spec, then a final code review with the engineering team before it lands in the library.

Key wins

What the system bought us

The system remains a work in progress, evolving through constant iteration — with regular feedback from the development team on what's working and where adjustments are needed. But the wins showed up quickly:

↗
Reduced development time

Engineers reuse existing components instead of coding from scratch — and the need for "quick calls" to clarify discrepancies dropped away.

↗
Cleaner design-to-dev handoff

Well-documented components with defined ownership of areas — teams find their first point of contact without asking around.

↗
Fewer QA cycles and revisions

Consistent coded components mean fewer misunderstandings and defects making it to review in the first place.

↗
A team to own it

The work led to a Global Web UI Team — engineers and designers collaborating to unify the systems across the whole product, with visual and functional consistency users can feel.

Reflections

A design system is a garden

Building the system wasn't a one-time fix — maintaining a design system is like maintaining a garden. As it grows, my goal is standardised, reusable assets that adapt across contexts and development frameworks, with documentation that earns its keep.

As the design team scales, I keep gathering feedback from new designers on how the system supports their workflows, streamlines onboarding, and maintains consistency — refining the documentation, usage guides, and instructions based on real-world insights from the team, not assumptions.
