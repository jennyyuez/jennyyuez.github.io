The challenge

A product hitting its ceiling

Construction is one of the world's least digitised industries — yet it carries some of the highest stakes. Safety, compliance, the coordination of hundreds of people across complex physical sites.

When I joined Sitemate as the first design hire, Dashpivot was early-stage and running into a hard ceiling. The wrong person could sign off on a safety form. Follow-up actions got chased over WhatsApp. Enterprise deals stalled because the product couldn't make the compliance guarantees regulated industries needed.

My job was to help turn Dashpivot from a useful tool into a platform people could stake their compliance on — while keeping it human enough for a site worker to use without training.

Feature story 01

Approval signatures & permissions

01
Compliance · Architecture · IA
Controlling who signs what — and what happens when they do
The problem

Anyone in Dashpivot could sign any form. For a SWMS or audit sign-off — documents with real safety and legal weight — that was a compliance failure waiting to happen. Enterprise prospects kept stalling. They needed to know the right person signed the right form at the right stage. The product couldn't guarantee that.

My approach

Solving this meant understanding the full architecture first — how permissions cascaded across folder levels, how internal and external users differed, and how Dashpivot's column-based workflow (Draft → In Progress → Complete) mapped to real approval chains on site. I created the IA for the permissions system, then designed a configurable logic field that let teams define exactly who could sign a form and what that signature triggered.

  • Signature fields became configurable — teams could define which permission type could sign, with multi-select support for complex approval chains
  • Permissions tiered across folder levels, with distinct rules for internal and external users
  • A signature could advance a form to the next column (approval) or reset it to the previous (rejection) — mirroring how approvals actually work on site
↗
Outcome
The feature unlocked enterprise deals that had previously stalled on compliance requirements — a key driver of the headcount growth that saw Sitemate double in year two.

Feature story 02

Actions — from WhatsApp to audit trail

02
Discovery · Workflow · Mobile
Replacing informal accountability with a documented, structured system
The problem

When a site manager found an issue during an audit, they'd send a WhatsApp message. Maybe it got actioned. Maybe it didn't. There was no way to know. Actions was the most heavily requested feature in the product — every construction document generates follow-up tasks, and without a system to track them, accountability fell through the gaps.

My approach

I ran discovery calls with customers before designing anything. The biggest insight: workers needed photo evidence — before and after — to prove a task was actually done, not just marked complete. That detail wouldn't have made it into scope without talking to people on site. I designed Actions as a workflow-native feature using Dashpivot's existing column model, so it felt familiar from day one.

  • V1 — Foundation: Core form with required fields (description, assignee, due date, priority), optional before photos, and a creator approval signature built on the permissions system already in place
  • V2 — Navigation: A dedicated Actions view for users to see everything they'd created or been assigned, with full status tracking across workflow columns
  • Later stages handed off to another team to continue building on the foundation
↗
Outcome
Site managers went from chasing people on WhatsApp to a full, searchable audit trail inside the same tool they already used for their documentation.

Design systems & IA

Making the invisible visible

With multiple product surfaces — web, mobile, integrations — and a team growing fast, consistency doesn't happen by accident. I built the design system with a modular approach: components flexible enough to support where the product was going, not just where it was.

I think about design at scale — not just how this works for one user, but how a single decision plays out across a hundred different contexts.

Reflections

What Sitemate taught me

Designing for construction workers — people whose daily reality involves physical risk, regulatory compliance, and often limited time with a screen — taught me that empathy isn't a soft skill. It's the most precise tool a designer has.

My path at Sitemate wasn't linear. Designer to designer/PM. Dashpivot to Flowsite to Foundations. Each move taught me something the previous one couldn't. I'm a better designer because I've sat in the PM seat. I'm a better PM because I understand how design decisions ripple through engineering.

If I'd do anything differently: more direct user research, earlier. Working closely with the CEO gave me a strong line to customer insight — but the moment in Actions where customers told us they needed photo proof of completion reminded me what you only get when you're talking to the person actually doing the job. I want more of those moments.
